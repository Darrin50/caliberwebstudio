<h2>Why Michigan's Rules Make This More Important Than In Most States</h2>

<p>Michigan is a corporate practice of medicine state. Med spas must operate as a Professional Corporation (PC) or Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC). The medical director — a licensed physician, PA, or nurse practitioner — must be actively involved in operations, not simply a name on a compliance certificate while someone else runs the clinical floor.</p>

<p>That structure also determines who can hold the needle. Only MDs, DOs, PAs, and NPs operating under a valid collaborative practice agreement with a physician may legally administer Botox in Michigan. An aesthetician cannot. A "certified injector" without a medical license cannot. A spa employee who attended a weekend training course cannot. Michigan's Bureau of Professional Licensing (LARA) maintains a public license search portal where you can verify any provider's credentials — name, license type, active status, and disciplinary record — in under two minutes.</p>

<p>This context matters because the price variance in metro Detroit is significant. <a href="https://alluremedical.com/btxfbmi/">Allure Medical</a>, with four metro Detroit locations, has offered Botox at $8/unit promotional pricing. <a href="https://www.michiganadvancedaesthetics.com/services/service-pricing/">Michigan Advanced Aesthetics in Royal Oak</a> charges $10–$20/unit. Specialty injectors in Birmingham and Grosse Pointe run $20+/unit. That spread reflects real differences in oversight structure, injector tenure, and what's included in the appointment — not just facility overhead. For a full breakdown of what drives Detroit's pricing range, see our guide on <a href="/blog/detroit-botox-cost-2026">what Botox costs in Detroit in 2026</a>.</p>

<h2>Question 1: Who Is Your Medical Director — and Are They Actually Involved?</h2>

<p>This is the most important structural question you can ask before booking anywhere in metro Detroit. A legitimate medical director is not a name on a framed certificate. They oversee treatment protocols, are reachable to injectors when complications arise, carry active liability for clinical outcomes, and — in a compliant Michigan practice — are either a physician, physician assistant, or nurse practitioner with a documented collaborative agreement in place.</p>

<p>When you contact a clinic, ask: "Who is your medical director, and what is their background in aesthetics?" A credible answer gives you a name, a license type, a specialty or training background, and some indication of how they engage with the practice day-to-day. A vague response — "we have a physician on file" without specifics — is worth following up on. If they're unwilling to name the director, that itself tells you something.</p>

<p>For context on what this looks like when it's done right: <a href="https://adornmedspa.com/">Adorn Medical Spa in Dearborn</a> has built 579 verified five-star Google reviews with NP Liz as the face of the practice and a transparently disclosed physician collaboration structure. That transparency is not just a compliance signal — it is the reason patients trust them with repeat appointments.</p>

<h2>Question 2: Who Will Physically Inject Me, and Can I Verify Their Michigan License?</h2>

<p>This is separate from the medical director question. The person holding the syringe may be the medical director — or may be an NP, PA, or RN working within the practice's supervision structure. Each license type carries different training requirements and different scope of practice in Michigan. You have a right to know who is performing the procedure before you consent to it.</p>

<p>What to ask: your injector's name, license type (MD, DO, PA, NP, or RN), Michigan license number, and how long they've been performing the specific treatment you're requesting. Then verify it. The LARA license search at <a href="https://w2.lara.michigan.gov/VAL/License/Search" rel="noopener noreferrer">w2.lara.michigan.gov</a> returns active status, license type, and disciplinary history on any Michigan licensed professional. An active license with no restrictions is the minimum bar — not a guarantee of skill, but a baseline you should never skip.</p>

<p>If a clinic is reluctant to provide the injector's name or license information before your appointment, that reluctance is an answer in itself.</p>

<h2>Question 3: What Does Your Consultation Process Look Like Before My First Appointment?</h2>

<p>A provider who books Botox the same way you'd book a blowout — pick a service, pick a time, pay — is skipping the step that determines whether your results will match your goals. Botox is a medical procedure. Treatment areas, unit counts, injection patterns, and product selection should come from a real clinical assessment, not a dropdown menu.</p>

<p>A proper consultation covers: your facial anatomy and muscle movement patterns, your aesthetic goals, realistic expectations for your baseline and skin type, how long results typically last for your age and muscle activity level, and what a follow-up or touch-up process looks like if you want adjustments. It should be a conversation with someone qualified to evaluate your face, not a form you fill out in the waiting room.</p>

<p>A consultation fee is not a red flag. Charging $50–$150 for a clinical evaluation with a licensed injector is appropriate practice, and many Detroit-area providers credit it toward treatment cost. A clinic that skips consultation entirely and jumps straight to booking raises a question worth asking: what assessment step are they replacing it with, and where does the clinical judgment about your specific anatomy happen?</p>

<h2>Question 4: Can I See Before-and-After Photos of Your Actual Patients?</h2>

<p>This is where you verify outcomes, not credentials. Michigan law requires patient consent before before-and-after photos can be published — a provider with a documented gallery has met that legal threshold. A gallery is also the clearest direct evidence of what results actually look like from this specific injector on real patients, not stock photography or results from training programs.</p>

<p>What to look for: consistent photography (same angle and similar lighting conditions across cases, not filtered or heavily edited), results shown at multiple weeks post-treatment rather than immediately after injection, and a range of skin tones and treatment areas that reflect the practice's actual patient base. A Botox gallery that shows only one skin type, or that shows only single-area results, may not demonstrate the depth of experience relevant to your specific treatment plan.</p>

<p>Detroit's patient population is majority Black in the city proper and significantly diverse across the metro. A clinic that cannot show results on melanin-rich skin types is not demonstrating experience with the anatomy and technique variations that matter for that patient profile. Ask specifically about cases similar to yours if the gallery doesn't immediately surface them.</p>

<p>Botox is a brand name — onabotulinumtoxinA manufactured by Allergan. Other FDA-approved neuromodulators used at Detroit-area clinics include Dysport (Galderma), Xeomin (Merz), and Jeuveau (Evolus). Each has a different molecular profile, spread pattern, and onset timeline. For the full clinical comparison — including which Detroit providers carry each and how the unit ratio translates to cost — the <a href="/blog/botox-vs-dysport-detroit">Botox vs. Dysport guide for Detroit patients</a> covers the differences in detail.</p>

<p>The brand question reveals something about the injector's clinical fluency. An injector who can explain why they prefer Botox for glabella injections but Dysport for large forehead areas — or who stocks Xeomin for patients who've developed resistance to other neurotoxins — is demonstrating clinical reasoning. "We just use Botox" without elaboration tells you less about their practice depth than you want to know before choosing them.</p>

<p>The touch-up policy question is equally direct. Botox takes 5–7 days to show results and up to two weeks to fully settle. An ethical injector has a documented policy for what happens if results are uneven, asymmetric, or don't match the treatment plan discussed in your consultation. You should know that policy before your appointment, not after you're sitting in front of a mirror wondering who to call.</p>

<h2>Question 7: How Long Have You Been Injecting This Specific Treatment Area?</h2>

<p>Botox experience is not uniform across treatment areas. Glabella (11s) injection is technically different from crow's feet, which is different from perioral lines, neck bands, lip flip, or jaw slimming with masseteric Botox. A provider who performs high-volume forehead Botox may have limited experience with the anatomical precision required for a jaw slimming treatment — or vice versa. Asking specifically about your treatment area gives you relevant information, not just general career length.</p>

<p>Ask how many treatments they perform per week in the area you want addressed, and how long they've been doing it. Ask whether they've managed complications in that area and how. Experience at volume — not just years on paper — is what produces pattern recognition in outcomes and the ability to course-correct when results drift from the plan.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions — Choosing a Botox Provider in Detroit</h2>

<h3>Can an aesthetician legally do Botox in Michigan?</h3>
<p>No. Michigan law restricts Botox to licensed MDs, DOs, PAs, and NPs operating under a valid collaborative practice agreement with a supervising physician. An aesthetician — regardless of certifications — cannot legally administer injectables in Michigan. If a clinic represents otherwise, that is a Michigan LARA compliance violation.</p>

<h3>How do I verify a Botox provider's license in Michigan?</h3>
<p>Use the LARA license verification portal at <a href="https://w2.lara.michigan.gov/VAL/License/Search" rel="noopener noreferrer">w2.lara.michigan.gov</a>. Search by the provider's name and license type (NP, PA, MD, or DO). The result shows active license status, license number, issue and expiration dates, and any disciplinary history. Active status with no restrictions is the minimum bar — verify this before your appointment, not after.</p>

<h3>Should I pay a consultation fee before my first Botox appointment?</h3>
<p>Yes — and a consultation fee is a positive signal, not a scheme. Charging $50–$150 for a clinical assessment is standard practice for credible injectors. It gets credited toward treatment at many Detroit-area clinics. A clinic that skips the consultation and books you directly without any clinical assessment step raises a more important question about where the clinical judgment happens.</p>

<h3>Is cheaper Botox in Detroit less safe?</h3>
<p>Not necessarily. Price is not a reliable safety proxy. An $8/unit promotional price at a multi-location clinic with board-certified supervision can be structurally safer than a $15/unit appointment at a solo-provider practice with no documented oversight. Evaluate credentials, consultation process, oversight structure, and documented results — not price alone. Use the seven questions above at every price point.</p>

<h3>What should I look for in a before-and-after gallery before choosing a Detroit Botox provider?</h3>
<p>Consistent photography across cases (same angle, similar lighting, not filtered), results shown at 2–4 weeks post-treatment rather than immediately after injection, a range of skin tones and treatment areas, and enough volume to demonstrate pattern — not just occasional standout results. A gallery with fewer than 20 Botox results across multiple areas suggests a relatively new or low-volume injector. Michigan law requires patient consent before publishing these photos, so a gallery also signals compliance with patient privacy requirements.</p>
